In their article ‘Digital inequalities and why they matter’ Laura Robinson et el establish that “digital inequality deserves a place alongside more traditional forms of inequality in the twenty-ﬁrst century pantheon of inequalities.” (Robinson et el 2015). They argue that the relevance and importance of digital inequalities are relevant in many areas of human social and personal aspects, throughout the “life course, gender, race, and class, as well as health care, politics, economic activity, and social capital.” (Robinson et el 2015). For this blog post, I will be focusing on the digital inequalities specific to gender.

Moyo Zoo Lake Restaurant

Notably, in an increasingly digital society an “ individuals’ digital engagements and digital capital play key roles in a range of outcomes, from academic performance to labour market success, to entrepreneurship, and health services uptake.” (Robinson et el 2015). This emphasizes the importance of equality regarding access to and the use of digital technologies, as well as, the internet. Robinson et el assert that “Those who function better in the digital realm and participate more fully in digitally mediated social life enjoy advantages over their digitally disadvantaged counterparts” (Robinson et el 2015). To give an illustration of this, one can refer to the current national pandemic and how it has impacted how business is conducted. Many companies and institutions have resorted to digital means to ensure the continuation of business and academic calendars, as a result, those who either lack the skill of using digital technologies or don’t have access to them, are placed at a disadvantage as compared to those who do.

Although there has been a decrease in the gap, between men and women, regarding access to digital technologies and the internet (Robinson et el 2015). There is still a significant difference in the advantages and use of the internet presented to them. Robinson et el, affirm that there is a difference in the frequency of use, the intensity of use, range of online activities, and the likelihood of creating strong internet skills in women as compared to men. Furthermore, they have discovered that recent evidence suggests that women are more likely to spend time on the internet for communication purposes and social support, reflecting how online behaviour is often “an extension of broader social roles, interests, and expectations existent in society.” (Robinson et el 2015).
